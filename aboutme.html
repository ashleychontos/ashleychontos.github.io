About me

Aloha and here I am with more clouds and mountains! As I was saying: I am a non-traditional, first-generation graduate student at the Institute for Astronomy at the University of Hawaii. I am pursuing my PhD in Astronomy under the advisement of Daniel Huber focusing on exoplanets that orbit asteroseismic stars. I am currently funded through the National Science Foundation Graduate Research Fellowship Program (NSF GRFP) and plan on proposing my thesis in March 2019 (yes, I know, I'm cursing myself).

I was born and raised in Woodstock, New York (yes, THE Woodstock). I've had a very non-linear trajectory into the field of astronomy beginning with a "gap" 7 years. As the first member of my family to go to college, I was unaware of the financial resources that are available to help students get through school. I was working full-time to support myself as a full-time student but it was too much (and burnout is very real). I decided to take time off in order to work and save up some money so I could return to school and not have to worry about working. I am an erstwhile model that was based out of NYC, Los Angeles, Tokyo, Beijing, and Seoul. This was the complete opposite end of the spectrum, as far as careers go. My love for science brought me back to school and now here I am! I am grateful for my experiences in the interim but I'm extremely happy to be doing science again.

Undergrad

I did my undergrad at the State University of New York at Albany, where I received my Bachelor's of Science in both Physics and Mathematics. I graduated Summa Cum Laude and collected a couple of unique research opportunities along the way.

In summer 2015, I had the privilege of working with Bruce Macintosh at Stanford University. Bruce is best known for his efforts in instrumentation in order to directly image exoplanets. He is principle investigator (PI) to GPI, the Gemini Planet Imager, and CO-PI to the WFIRST (Wide-Field InfraRed Survey Telescope) coronagraph instrument (CGI). The work Bruce and I did that summer is explained here. I was also allowed to tag along with him and three of his postdocs on an observing run at the Gemini South Telescope in La Serena, Chile, where GPI currently resides. As you can see on the right, we really love GPI (pictured from left to right: Kate Follette - now at Amherst, Eric Nielsen, Bruce Macintosh, Vanessa Bailey - now at JPL, me). I can say fairly confidently that my work with Bruce cultivated my love for exoplanetary science.

The timing worked out perfectly that I could also attend a data science school that same summer while I was in Chile (it was actually the other way around but those are minor details). The workshop was the "Third La Serena School for Data Science: Applied Tools for Astronomy" and seems pretty self-explanatory. In case you're really curious about it.
